Reaction to Jill Freedman's Theatre of the Streets (link).

 

The photographer's work typically features human subjects. In a variety of situations, through her lens the audience is shown depictions of the grittiest parts of America’s urban landscapes through the 1960s and 70s.

 

Among my favorites of Freedman’s pieces from the exhibit, as well as those that most directly inspired my response video (“haircut”), were those that featured the most charismatic subjects in the most bizarre environments. These include “Teasing the Cops” (1979), “South Bronx Dining” (1976), “An End to Nixon” (1976), “Greenwich Village Couple” (1973), “Alphabet City” (1980), and “Times Square” (1979).

 

These photographs include (in no particular order): two women sticking their tongues out at a New York City police officer, a little girl eating a sandwich atop a wrecked and stripped-out truck, a malaise-era land-yacht reverse-parked against a concrete wall marked with a long strip of graffiti, two eccentric-looking lesbians, a man wearing what appears to be a crown of thorns and a sad expression waving a Puerto Rican flag on a sidewalk in Alphabet City, and a fashionably-dressed Black man adorned in round glasses, a fitted button-down shirt fastened down below his mid-chest, and a necklace. The last subject is posed flamboyantly in front of a what appears to be a jewelry store in Times Square with his lips puckered large and his hands in the air.

 

Also, all of the photos featured in the exhibit were taken in black & white.

 

Response

 

My own reactions to those named photographs ranged from heartbreak to hilarity. Others simply push boundaries in a way that brings on feelings of righteousness – like “Teasing the Cops,” which is as silly as it is scary. Even so, it certainly makes me smile. “Greenwich Village Couple,” too, I can certainly appreciate for its friendly depiction of what would have been considered extreme counterculture in the 1970s. “An End to Nixon,” which depicts more politics than any of the others, also makes me feel sort of pointlessly smug. Images like these ones make me feel nostalgic for a time I never lived through.

 

Others, like I said, are more overtly tragic, and cause feelings accordingly. “South Bronx Dining” for instance, makes me feel bad for the poor girl on top of the car. The photo is ominous, and forces the audience to ask terrible questions like, “where are this girl’s parents?” Looking at it made a pit rise in my stomach. It’s certainly the featured photograph that stuck with me the most.

 

Photos like “Alphabet City” and “Times Square” are unfortunate for the separate reason that, whether the subjects know it or not, the places they’re standing in are on the verge of a massive socio-economic shift that will have serious implications for their residents including displacement and homelessness. These photos make me feel sad.

 

Interpretation

 

My interpretation for the exhibit is focused less on the photographs from the late 1960s featuring images from the Poor People’s Campaign and Vietnam Protests, as I think they’re different enough from the later New York photographs to justify their own separate interpretation.

 

Speaking primarily for those 1970s photos set in New York City, I think these images tell the story of a place in transition, or, from a contemporary perspective, how things were before.

 

The final two photos, “Alphabet City” (1980) and “Times Square” (1979) clearly depict single men-of-color standing proudly in two of the Manhattan neighborhoods that changed the most over the course of the 70s and 80s, and both photos clearly use the word “gentrification” in their description plaques posted beside them.

 

All together, these photos, as well as another set in Times Square titled “Peep Show, New York City” (1986) all describe a grittier version of the New York City we know today, one with obvious poverty, slums, adult movie theatres, and sleazy jewelry stores. They serve to recognize the former inhabitants of these now-changed places and to pay homage to the people that modern economic forces have displaced.

 

“haircut”

 

I recorded my response piece in effort to create an example of what “theatre of the streets” might look like in 2022. The video depicts a symbolic reclamation of a public space (the sidewalk in front of our house) for public use.

 

Set in our front “yard” in Northampton, Massachusetts, it includes visuals of a bleached-blonde, earring-wearing boy wearing sweatpants and dirty Crocs sitting in a barber’s chair on the sidewalk while his girlfriend tries to shave his head, as well as some elements of the surrounding environment.

 

Set in what should appear to be a wealthy residential neighborhood, the camera pans to several commonly recognized symbols of classlessness: cigarette butts, graffiti, shoes without socks, and a plastic crustacean hanged from a fishing line in-front of the front door.

 

The video cuts between all these visuals until the sudden appearance of a faceless dirt-bike rider, who inconsiderately starts his un-muffled scooter in broad daylight, blocking the shot of the haircut.

 

Formatted as a compilation of hand-held camera shots lasting between 1-2 seconds, the response piece is meant to come across as unpretentious, informal, and raw. The lack of music or speech is supposed to make the audience feel a little discomfort while they watch and should further contribute to a sense of truthful realism.
